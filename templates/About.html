ABOUT FIRE EYE

Fire Eye is the final year project of Fatima Jinnah Women University of Pakistan providing with a real-time forest fire and smoke detection and alarm system to detect the occurrence of forest fires and smoke at an early stage, and to alert firefighters and first responders to take immediate action to prevent the spread of the fire and minimize damage to life and property. The FireEye includes, starting from pre-fire state, the following modules and technology:

  • Drones based surveillance to discover, locate and confirm fire ignition
  • GPS module to provide exact location of the detected fire
  • Active fire detection module to detect specific patterns that indicate fire presence
  • Alert module to monitor the system's status and view fire alerts in real-time
  • Help firefighters through enhanced situational awareness
  • User-friendly system for easy wildfire management and monitoring

OUR GOALS

WHAT FIREEYE IS DOING?

Healthy forest ecosystems are critical for our communities and climate action. Forests are the lungs of our earth, helping to keep our climate stable by purifying the air as they absorb carbon and produce  oxygen, which is essential for human life on earth. FireEye is working to protect and restore forests, end deforestation, and expand sustainable forest management to help the people and wildlife that depend on them.

FORESTS ARE THE ROOT OF ALL LIFE

Pakistan has 4.2 million ha of forest and planted trees, which equates to 4.8% of the total land area. The forestry sector of Pakistan is the main source for lumber, paper, fuelwood, latex, and medicines. Five hundred thousand workers are employed in forestry and forest-related industries such as logging, village carpentry, and the manufacture of timber components for the construction industry. Forests supply 32% of Pakistan’s total energy needs in the form of fuelwood. Forests also provide forage for one third of Pakistan’s 86 million head of livestock. Leather, wood, and other livestock products contribute US$400 million, or 9%, of total export earnings. In short, forests play an important role in providing shelter, as over 2 billion people rely on them. As a source of food, shelter, water, fuel, and security, there is a greater need to preserve our forests. According to the FAO, there were 32.0k people employed in Pakistan's forest sector in 2010, which is a major reason to think about the preservation of forests.

DEFORESTATION – A PRE-DECIDED DEVASTATION

Deforestation is a major environmental concern in the world. Deforestation includes the cutting down, burning, and destructing of forests. It is the first link in a chain of environmental degradation that includes erosion, climate change, air pollution, and the loss of hardwood and fuel wood. Forests are being cut down in Pakistan at a petrifying rate. The one of the major causes of climate change in Pakistan is deforestation. In Pakistan, the agriculture, forestry and land-use sectors are responsible for 22% of global greenhouse gas emissions, and deforestation is a major driver. In Pakistan, forest fires were responsible for 56% of tree cover loss between 2001 and 2021. Out of which 4.6% of tree cover loss occurred in areas where the dominant drivers of loss resulted in Deforestation.

PROTECT FOREST TO COMBAT CLIMATE CHANGE

Forests are a climate hero, buffering communities against extreme weather and absorbing and locking away planet-warming carbon dioxide. They are valuable carbon repositories and play an important role for the climate. When forests are destroyed, they release large quantities of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, which further exacerbates the climate crisis. It takes decades to reforest areas ravaged by wildfires. From 2001 to 2021, Pakistan lost 9.75kha of tree cover, equivalent to a 1.0% decrease in tree cover since 2000, and 3.56Mt of CO₂ emissions. Currently, Pakistan is facing adverse and severe effects of climate crisis in the shape of extreme temperatures, severe drought and forest fires. Fire Eye is here to ensure nature-based solutions deliver for people and nature, by providing expert guidance and support on monitoring, reporting and verification of emissions that truly benefit Indigenous peoples and local communities as well as the climate and forests.
